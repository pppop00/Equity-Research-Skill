I. Investment summary

NVIDIA is the leading accelerated-computing platform company: its GPUs, networking, and CUDA software anchor a large share of training and inference workloads for generative AI, recommender systems, and scientific computing. FY2026 revenue reached about $216 billion, up roughly 65% year over year, driven overwhelmingly by Data Center demand as hyperscalers and enterprises expanded AI infrastructure.

Profitability remains exceptional versus semiconductor peers—GAAP gross margin stepped down to near 71% from about 75% in the prior fiscal year as mix and ramp costs mattered more at scale, while operating margin held above 60% and free cash flow approached $97 billion. Cash and strategic securities provide flexibility for R&D, ecosystem investments, and shareholder returns even as capex rises.

The primary debates for FY2027 are whether growth normalizes after a historic capex cycle, how China export controls reshape the revenue path (management‘s Q1 outlook explicitly assumes no Data Center compute revenue from China), and whether custom silicon gradually compresses pricing power. Supply of advanced packaging and memory can still gate shipments even when end demand is strong.

Key highlights

  • Record FY2026 revenue (~$216B) with Data Center contributing the vast majority of the mix.
  • Very strong operating and free cash flow conversion; balance sheet leverage remains modest.
  • Full-stack positioning (chips, systems, networking, software) supports developer lock-in and faster enterprise adoption.
  • Clear roadmap messaging around Blackwell/Vera Rubin platforms sustains upgrade cadence among major customers.

Key risks

  • Geopolitical/export-control risk: outlook explicitly excludes China Data Center compute—policy changes can move revenue faster than fundamentals.
  • Customer concentration: a small set of large cloud buyers can pressure pricing, allocation, and payment terms.
  • Competition from in-house accelerators (ASIC/TPU-class programs) and AMD’s MI-series roadmap.
  • Execution risk in supply chain (CoWoS/HBM/packaging) and potential margin volatility during product transitions.
Investment thesis: We view NVIDIA as the default ‘picks-and-shovels’ exposure to enterprise AI capex with a durable software ecosystem moat—but we also assume more competition and policy friction as the market matures. The stock tends to price rapid growth far forward; our focus is on gross margin stabilization, China policy clarity, and evidence that revenue remains supply-constrained rather than demand-limited.
